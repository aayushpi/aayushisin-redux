Messaging with intent {A work in progress}

When you get past the small talk,
communication is always about intent.

We live our lives connected to more people than ever before. And quite often, our interaction is occasional, across many disconnected channels. This makes finding time to engage spend with these connections hard.

Or to put it a different way, there’s a difference between wanting to and making things happen: (also known as the San Francisco Yes)

On the surface, DoNext may not seem related to this: after all, it looks and acts like a productivity app. However, by bringing elements that keep you on point from the productivity world (calendars, reminders, to-dos) and integrating it into a messaging system, it becomes a powerful (and accountable) way to make things happen: texting someone to ‘get coffee’ is now instantly an event: every person’s availability is checked, and then added across their calendars. Changes to time and location are synced. A common checklist is shared. Finally, everyone’s on the same page.

DoNext has many, incredibly forward thinking ideas that are just about ready for release: intelligent automatic messaging if you’re running late, talking to the Internet of Things, and much more. Stay tuned.

My Role

I’m the lead (read: only) product designer on DoNext, and thus my responsibilities include the app, marketing collateral and other visual assets. I’m also involved in the day-to-day development of the web prototype, and often add hotfixes and even the occasional feature to the Sinatra backend. Recently, I’ve also taken up a significant amount of responsibility in promoting and distributing the app. While this spread worries me for the inability to really dwell on something completely, I’ve accepted it as a nature of doing what is most important.

To mitigate risks and validate decisions, I also spend a lot of time prototyping and using analytics for insights: thus, I spend a lot of time implementing multi-variate tests via Mixpanel, Google Analytics and Ruby/JavaScript.

Designing DoNext

DoNext is a delightful challenge to design around. By nature, we’re combining an asynchronous messaging system with elements from Calendars and reminders which necessitate synchronization. Conveying what happens when you press a button becomes crucial when you’re introducing new metaphors: often necessitating custom controls, just-in-time tutorials, and many interations of interface copywriting

To balance the needs of these new directions with the realities that come in a two-person team (myself + a developer), my approach has been to create a modular, highly editable design system that I can go back to and change easily. That’s why most of DoNext is designed on Sketch:

To expedite development, I’ve created a component library that’s constantly updated (which helps easy export of assets) and contains every block of the app, in every possible state (highlighted, active, inactive, error... the works). This helps in many ways: quick screenshots for marketing collateral, or to try a new direction is as easy as draggging and dropping LEGO blocks. And over the long run, it gives a great birds-eye view of the design system: what works, what doesn’t, what looks dated. This allows for components themselves to be constantly updated as needed without requiring big redesign or refactor sprints - again, extremely important in a one-person design team.

Before an idea is committed to code though, there’s a significant amount of prototyping involved: I usually do this via Marvel or XCode (while testing for flow) or Quartz Composer (while testing for fidelity).

In general, I like to expose ideas early and often: however, for a number of reasons, this was not possible with DoNext: my strategy then involved using a large number of events stored to capture user states and transitions (this was done via Mixpanel), and creating targeted email and push notifications to verify if users understood the system. I also helped implement a progressive reduction on the UI where features resolved to their final look after users completely understood the flow and intention of each action.
